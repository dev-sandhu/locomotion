Energy Supply and Environmental Factors

Energy supply
Muscle movement requires a substantial supply of energy in the form of ATP. Vertebrates primarily utilize phosphocreatine to meet this demand, while invertebrates employ a combination of phosphoarginine, phosphoplycocyamine, phosphotaurocyamine, or phospholambricine. There are two primary pathways that contribute to locomotion: anaerobic glycolysis and mitochondrial aerobic metabolism. Each pathway plays a distinct role in facilitating movement.
Muscle structure and function vary across different groups of organisms, with notable distinctions between invertebrates and vertebrates.
In the case of invertebrates, many of them employ a unique approach to movement known as the hydrostatic skeleton. This system combines simple muscles with fluid-filled internal chambers. Unlike vertebrates, invertebrates exhibit a different organization of striated muscles. For example, earthworms possess both circular and longitudinal striated muscles, which, in conjunction with their segmented body, allow them to generate peristaltic waves. This specialized movement pattern offers precise control over their locomotion.
Squids, another group of invertebrates, feature a distinct muscle arrangement. They have two planes of muscle layers: radial and circular muscles. When the radial muscles contract, the thickness and circumference of the mantle reduce, enabling jet propulsion. This unique adaptation makes squids the fastest aquatic invertebrates.
In vertebrates, the types of muscle fibers and their functions vary depending on the specific requirements of the organism. Fish, for instance, have two main types of muscle fibers: red and white. White muscle is responsible for high-intensity swimming, such as rapid escape from predators. Red muscle, characterized by its oxidative properties, is used for slow and steady swimming, like cruising. Some fish also possess pink muscle, which falls between the contractile properties of red and white muscles.
Tetrapods, which include amphibians, reptiles, and mammals, employ a combination of muscles that work cooperatively to achieve coordinated movement. Many muscles in tetrapods function antagonistically, where one group of muscles relaxes while another contracts. An example of this is the coordination of antagonistic muscles in the limbs. Additionally, to maintain posture and balance, muscles in the back and abdomen coordinate their actions.
Birds exhibit a unique muscular arrangement, especially in the context of powered flight. Their muscles work antagonistically to power the wings. During the downstroke, the pectoralis muscle contracts, while during the upstroke, the supracoracoideus muscle contracts. In the case of birds like hummingbirds, which hover, the supracoracoideus muscle is notably developed to assist in rapidly lifting the wings. The fine control of wing movements in birds is achieved through the coordination of more than 45 different muscles, allowing for precise and agile flight maneuvers. Oxidative phosphorylation is a highly efficient process that yields 34 more ATP molecules per glucose molecule compared to glycolysis. Consequently, relaxing and slow-twitch muscles, such as the red muscles in fish, rely on oxidative phosphorylation to meet their energy requirements. This method ensures sustained energy production during prolonged or less intense muscle activity.
In contrast, glycolysis is capable of generating ATP more rapidly than oxidative phosphorylation. This characteristic makes it the preferred pathway for quick bursts of movement. However, one limitation of glycolysis is the limited storage of glycogen, which serves as the primary source of glucose for this process. This limitation can constrain the supply of ATP during intense or prolonged activities.
Furthermore, glycolysis can function in the absence of oxygen, making it a valuable resource when oxygen delivery to muscles cannot keep pace with high-intensity exercise. This scenario results in a functional oxygen deficit in the muscle, prompting reliance on stored glycogen for lactate production.
In terms of substrate utilization, glycolysis primarily depends on carbohydrates, while the mitochondria possess the versatility to utilize carbohydrates, lipids, and amino acids to produce ATP. This adaptability allows the organism to tap into various energy sources, depending on its immediate requirements and available resources.

Oxygen Delivery to Muscles
The delivery of oxygen to muscles is a crucial aspect of an organism's physiology, and it varies across different species, depending on their activity levels and environmental adaptations.
In simpler organisms like nematodes and flatworms, oxygen delivery is achieved through simple gas diffusion. These organisms have relatively low metabolic demands and can rely on the passive movement of oxygen through their tissues. However, more active animals with higher metabolic rates require specialized cardiovascular systems to efficiently transport oxygen to their muscles and remove metabolic waste products, such as carbon dioxide.
One fascinating example of oxygen delivery optimization is observed in ray-finned fish, where the "Root effect" plays a critical role. The Root effect is a physiological phenomenon in which a decrease in pH (an increase in protons) in the blood reduces hemoglobin's affinity and carrying capacity for oxygen. This adjustment benefits the delivery of oxygen to the tissues of ray-finned fish. The decrease in pH within fish tissues primarily results from the production of carbon dioxide, which subsequently leads to a reduction in hemoglobin-oxygen affinity, thereby increasing blood oxygen levels and enhancing oxygen unloading in the tissues. Carbonic anhydrase, present on blood vessels, plays a pivotal role in this process by converting protons and bicarbonate into carbon dioxide, which then diffuses into red blood cells. Inside these cells, carbon dioxide is transformed back into protons and bicarbonate, indirectly increasing the acidity within the cells, thus allowing for increased unloading of oxygen.
Myoglobin is another vital component of oxygen delivery, particularly in aerobic muscles. Myoglobin is a heme protein responsible for transporting and storing oxygen within muscle cells. It ensures that oxygen is readily available for muscle contraction and energy production during periods of increased activity.
Diving mammals, such as whales and seals, experience hypoxic conditions during prolonged dives and need to maintain high myoglobin concentrations in their muscles. Myoglobin plays a critical role in enabling these mammals to store and transport sufficient oxygen to support their underwater activities. The ability to store oxygen in this manner helps diving mammals thrive in their challenging aquatic environments.

Environmental Factors
Environmental factors, particularly gravity and fluid properties, are significant influences on animal locomotion. The means by which animals navigate these factors vary depending on their habitat and physiology.
Gravity:
Gravity exerts its greatest influence on terrestrial animals, as they must utilize their muscles to overcome the obstacles associated with gravitational pull. The need to support their own body weight and move against gravity requires significant energy expenditure. In contrast, aquatic animals living at the bottom of bodies of water tend to have denser bodies, which prevents them from floating. However, even for aquatic animals like fishes, buoyancy is essential to enable movement in water. Buoyancy acts as a counteracting force to gravity, allowing these creatures to move with less energy expenditure compared to their terrestrial counterparts.
Fluid Mechanics:
Moving through fluids, such as water or air, presents a unique set of challenges due to the patterns of flow created by these media. Animals navigating through fluids must manage the surrounding medium to create their own path effectively. This principle holds true both for swimming in water and flying in the air.
The shape of an animal is a crucial component in overcoming drag, the force that opposes forward movement in a fluid. For swimmers, having a streamlined shape resembling a teardrop is the most efficient design. This shape minimizes drag and reduces the energy required for propulsion. Additionally, the surface of the animal's body and the presence of fins play a significant role in facilitating movement through fluid.
In the case of flying animals, such as birds, wings serve a dual purpose for both flapping and hovering flight. The size of the wings significantly influences flight capabilities. Birds with larger wings, like albatrosses, are well-suited for soaring, thanks to their extensive wing surface area. Feathers on the wings assist in guiding the flow of air, which is crucial for maintaining stability and control during flight.
Amphibians, such as frogs and toads, undergo metamorphosis. Tadpoles, in their aquatic phase, swim using a tail and can thrust forward using their trunk. As they develop limbs, they acquire the ability to both swim and jump. The length and capacity for rapid muscle contraction in frogs enable them to achieve impressive jumping heights.
On the other hand, fleas possess a unique mechanism for jumping. Their muscles do not directly power the jump; instead, the muscles pull and release an internal spring, enabling the flea to leap hundreds of times its own body size.
