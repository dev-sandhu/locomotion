UTI Information

Urinary tract infections (UTIs) are frequent, recurrent infections that range in severity from non-life-threatening to life-threatening. The flaws in our existing treatment paradigm for UTIs have been highlighted by the continuous growth of antibiotic resistance and our growing understanding of the negative effects conferred by broad-spectrum antibiotic use on the health of the beneficial microbiota of the host. In this Review, we explain how recent research in the fields of microbiology, structural biology, genetics, and immunology has improved our knowledge of host-pathogen interactions throughout the pathogenesis of the urinary tract. The strategy for treating UTIs may change as a result of these fundamental scientific discoveries, moving away from broad-spectrum antibiotics that target conserved aspects of bacterial replication and toward pathogen-specific antibiotic-sparing therapeutics that target core determinants of bacterial virulence at the host-pathogen interface. Uropathogenic Escherichia coli (UPEC), which accounts for over 80% of urinary tract infections (UTIs), is a prevalent illness. In the United States, more than 10% of females and 3% of males have a physician-diagnosed UTI each year, and more than 60% of females will have a UTI at some point in their lifetime.  A person's risk of infection varies according on their age, sexual activity, family history, medical comorbidities, and past UTI history, among other things.  UTIs, which are defined as three or more UTIs in a calendar year More than 30% of women may get a recurrent infection within a year of the resolution of their initial symptoms, so factors like significantly contribute to the incidence and morbidity rates of UTI.
