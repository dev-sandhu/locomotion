Organ Systems

Digestive System
The process of finding, consuming, and digesting food is a fundamental aspect of an organism's physiology. To begin, organisms employ various sensory mechanisms to locate food sources. These mechanisms involve signaling pathways, where receptors interact with specific ligands, enabling the detection of certain chemicals. For example, gustatory receptors are employed to detect the presence of particular chemicals. Additionally, organisms can use their senses to identify energy sources in the form of light, sound, heat, or electricity. For instance, sharks are capable of detecting chemicals present in vertebrate blood. Predators often rely on their ability to sense the warmth and sounds produced by nearby prey. Locomotion is another essential aspect of an organism's search for food. While various senses help detect food, the ability to move towards a food source is crucial. An example of this is tape worms, which migrate in the gastrointestinal tracts and follow the release of nutrients from digested food.
Once food is located, it can be consumed through different mechanisms. In some organisms, like sponges that lack mouths, food is engulfed using phagocytosis. Water flows through the sponge's pores, where particles are ingested and digestion occurs within the cells. For organisms with mouths, various methods are employed. Cnidarians, for example, use their tentacles to transfer prey into their mouths, where digestion takes place in the gastrodermal cells in the gastrovascular cavity. In more advanced animals, limbs or other body parts are used to transfer food to the mouth or bring the mouth closer to the food source. Certain animals, such as squid, wasps, and spiders, possess hardened mouth parts that help mechanically break down the food's surface. Vertebrates have paired jaws for this purpose.
In mammals, the gastrointestinal (GI) tract is a complex system responsible for digestion. Proteins, complex sugars, and lipids are hydrolyzed into their basic components â€“ amino acids, monosaccharides, and fatty acids. These end products are released into the cytoplasm before being taken into cells with the help of various digestive enzymes. To optimize this process, the GI tract has specialized structures. Villi, finger-like extensions on the gut's surface, maximize the surface area for absorption. The intestinal lining contains microvilli, further increasing the surface area. This extensive surface area facilitates efficient absorption of nutrients.
Salivary glands located in the mouth play a critical role in the initial breakdown of food. These glands secrete enzymes that initiate the digestive process, helping to break down food components into smaller, more digestible units. This is just the beginning of a complex physiological process that ensures the body receives the necessary nutrients for energy, growth, and maintenance. The digestive system, therefore, is a central component of an organism's physiology, responsible for extracting essential resources from the environment to support life and well-being.

Respiratory System
The respiratory system is an important part of the body's physiology since it allows gases to pass between the environment and our cells. The circulation of air through the body, the diffusion of gases across various body surfaces, and the subsequent transfer of these gases into the circulatory system are all part of this intricate process. It is crucial to highlight that different creatures have evolved distinct systems for achieving successful gas exchange, depending on their environment and specialized demands.
Aquatic creatures, for example, have evolved unidirectional ventilation systems mostly using gills. Water runs in one direction over the gills of these organisms, ensuring a constant and effective supply of oxygen. This adaptation is critical for their survival in water environments because it maximizes oxygen intake while decreasing the possibility of oxygen-depleted areas within the gill chambers. This unidirectional flow of water allows oxygen to be extracted from the water and carried into the circulatory system to be given to the cells. Carbon dioxide, another waste product of metabolism, is similarly efficiently taken from the circulatory system and released back into the environment via the gills.
Terrestrial species, on the other hand, have evolved various respiratory architecture to meet their needs. To ensure gas exchange, they commonly use tidally ventilated lungs or air-filled tubes. Air is taken into the respiratory organs and then released in a cyclic pattern in this system. The lungs, or air-filled tubes, contain a vast surface area that is necessary for oxygen and carbon dioxide diffusion. The tidal ventilation technique ensures a constant supply of fresh air, which allows for effective gas exchange. The same system absorbs oxygen into the bloodstream and eliminates carbon dioxide.
Sponges, a distinct group of aquatic animals, use an unusual method of gas exchange. They do not have specialized respiratory structures such as gills or lungs. Instead, sponges rely on choanocytes, which are specialized cells with flagella. Water currents are created by choanocytes moving water through the pores of the sponge and into its central cavity. As water passes through, oxygen diffuses into the sponge's cells, providing the oxygen required for metabolism, while carbon dioxide diffuses out as a waste product. This simple but effective mechanism allows sponges to efficiently absorb oxygen from their surroundings and expel carbon dioxide.
Cnidarians, such as jellyfish and corals, have their own gas exchange system. These organisms lack respiratory structures as well as specialized organs such as lungs and gills. Instead, they rely on muscle contractions to move water into their mouth and gastrovascular cavity, from which it is distributed throughout the body. As water circulates through the cnidarian's body, oxygen diffuses into the cells, meeting their metabolic needs, while carbon dioxide diffuses out and is eventually expelled into the surrounding water. This mechanism allows cnidarians to keep the necessary gas exchange in their aquatic environment.

Circulatory System
The circulatory system is an essential component of the physiology of various organisms, as it transports essential fluids throughout the body. It is a dynamic network of structures and mechanisms that keep fluid, typically blood, flowing within the organism. This system ensures the distribution of nutrients, gases, and other necessary substances, as well as the removal of waste. Circulatory system characteristics vary between species, ranging from simple arrangements to more complex, closed systems.
In its most basic form, a circulatory system consists of four fundamental components: a pump (typically a heart in vertebrates and invertebrates), propulsive structures that drive the fluid flow, a network of spaces or vessels through which the fluid can circulate, and the fluid itself. It is important to note that not all organisms possess a circulatory system; simpler creatures like sponges and cnidarians have alternative mechanisms for fluid movement.
Sponges, for instance, utilize specialized cells equipped with flagellae to rhythmically move water through their bodies. This water circulation serves multiple purposes, including the exchange of gases and nutrients, and the removal of waste products. Cnidarians, on the other hand, employ a unique mechanism whereby they allow water to enter through their gastrovascular cavity. Subsequently, they pump this water, which carries digested food and oxygen, to their tentacles. This process plays a crucial role in nutrient distribution and respiration within these organisms.
A common distinction among circulatory systems lies in whether they are open or closed. Most mollusks and many insects possess open circulatory systems. In an open system, the circulating fluid, which is typically hemolymph, directly contacts the organism's tissues. Consequently, there is direct mixing of the circulating fluid with cellular fluids. This open arrangement facilitates the exchange of nutrients and other essential substances between the circulatory system and the cells. In these systems, the heart pumps hemolymph into open spaces, and its flow interacts directly with the surrounding tissues.
In contrast, annelids and vertebrates, including humans, exhibit closed circulatory systems. In a closed system, the fluid (blood) remains within enclosed blood vessels at all times, without direct contact with the surrounding tissues. Substances such as oxygen, nutrients, and waste products must diffuse through the vessel walls to enter or exit the tissues. For example, in the case of fish, their closed circulatory system involves the heart pumping blood from the heart through the gills, where oxygen is obtained, and then distributing it to the various tissues before it eventually returns to the heart. This separation between the circulatory fluid and the tissues enables precise regulation of circulation and maintains the integrity of the internal environment.
The heart has four chambers: two atria (upper chambers) and two ventricles (lower chambers). Blood flows from the body into the right atrium, then into the right ventricle, which pumps it to the lungs for oxygenation. Oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium, moves to the left ventricle, and is pumped out to the rest of the body. The atria receive blood, while the ventricles pump it, ensuring a continuous circulation.
1. Right Atrium (RA):Deoxygenated blood returns to the heart from the body through the superior and inferior vena cava and enters the right atrium.
2. Tricuspid Valve:This valve separates the right atrium from the right ventricle. When the right atrium contracts, blood passes through the tricuspid valve into the right ventricle.
3. Right Ventricle (RV):The right ventricle contracts, pumping deoxygenated blood through the pulmonary valve into the pulmonary artery.
4. Pulmonary Artery:The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs, where it picks up oxygen and releases carbon dioxide.
5. Left Atrium (LA):Oxygenated blood returns to the heart from the lungs through the pulmonary veins and enters the left atrium.
6. Bicuspid or Mitral Valve:This valve separates the left atrium from the left ventricle. When the left atrium contracts, blood passes through the mitral valve into the left ventricle.
7. Left Ventricle (LV):The left ventricle contracts, pumping oxygenated blood through the aortic valve into the aorta.
8. Aorta: The aorta is the largest artery in the body and carries oxygenated blood to various parts of the body, supplying nutrients and oxygen.

This entire process constitutes the cardiac cycle, and the coordinated contraction and relaxation of the heart chambers maintain a continuous flow of blood through the circulatory system. The valves ensure a one-way flow, preventing backflow of blood. This rhythmic pumping action is vital for supplying oxygen and nutrients to the body's tissues and organs (Sainsbury, 1922).
